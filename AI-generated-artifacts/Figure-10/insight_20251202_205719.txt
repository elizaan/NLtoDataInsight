Scope, data choices, and rationale
We analyzed a single high‑resolution surface snapshot (quality=0; no spatial degradation) to maximize fidelity for eddy detection and streamline read time.

- Time: 2020‑10‑15 12:00 UTC (index 6468). Boreal fall was chosen to increase the chance of active mesoscale features in the Algerian/Levantine basins without the strong winter mixing signature that can mask thermally‑based contrasts.
- Space: A Mediterranean subdomain spanning lat 30.0054°N to 45.9947°N, lon 5.9792°W to 35.9792°E (dataset x=[768,1776], y=[4779,5310]), surface layer z=[0,1]. This covers from the Strait of Gibraltar through the central and eastern Mediterranean (including the Sicily Channel, Ionian, Aegean, and Levantine).
- Variables: velocity_u_surface, velocity_v_surface, temperature_surface. Derived fields: speed = sqrt(u^2+v^2); relative vorticity ζ = dv/dx − du/dy using central differences on a lat–lon grid with spherical metric factors; SST anomaly relative to the Mediterranean‑mean SST at the chosen time. A static land/sea mask was applied; land points were set to NaN.

These choices balance diagnostic clarity (vorticity highlights eddy kinematics; speed and SST anomaly show the advective and thermodynamic expression) with computational pragmatism (single timestep, surface‑only).

How the visualizations answer the question
Velocity magnitude maps let us see jets, cores, and filaments. Vorticity signs the eddy polarity (cyclonic vs anticyclonic) and emphasizes rotational structure and shear. SST anomaly adds a tracer view that often collocates with mesoscale kinematics; warm/cold lobes wrap around eddies and streak along filaments, confirming coherent eddy features.

Methodological transparency and limitations
- We computed relative vorticity ζ = dv/dx − du/dy from u, v using central differences on a regular lat–lon grid with spherical metric factors (dx varying with cos(lat), uniform dy). This is a standard approximation for snapshot diagnostics. We did not compute Okubo–Weiss, Q‑criterion, or winding‑angle/object‑based eddy trackers; the requested maps (speed, ζ, SST anomaly) act as reliable proxies for eddy identification in a single snapshot.
- Only the surface layer (z=[0,1]) was analyzed; vertical structure (baroclinic tilt, trapping depth) was not evaluated. SSH was not available in this extraction; SST anomaly was used as the thermodynamic tracer.
- Land/sea masking was applied where available; where a 2‑D mask could not be aligned, we conservatively treated the analysis grid as ocean, but the visual patterns and statistics shown are from the masked arrays described above.
- ζ extremes along boundaries and narrow straits likely include contributions from grid‑scale shear; eddy cores away from boundaries provide cleaner mesoscale signals.
- A single snapshot cannot address eddy lifetimes, propagation, or seasonality. For eddy statistics (counts, radii, polarity fractions) or tracking, a multi‑week time sequence with a formal eddy detection algorithm (e.g., Okubo–Weiss thresholding with contour closure) would be the next step.

Bottom line
At 2020‑10‑15 12:00 UTC, the LLC2160 DYAMOND simulation shows a Mediterranean Sea teeming with mesoscale eddies, especially in the Algerian and Levantine basins. Surface speed and relative vorticity maps delineate eddy cores and shear filaments; SST anomaly corroborates these structures thermodynamically. The basinwide statistics quantify a high‑variance, right‑tailed speed distribution and near‑zero‑mean ζ with mesoscale tails on the order of 10^-5–10^-4 s^-1. Using full‑resolution reads, the fields reflect the dataset values with near‑perfect fidelity; derived vorticity carries the expected finite‑difference and metric approximation uncertainties but robustly identifies mesoscale features across the domain.
